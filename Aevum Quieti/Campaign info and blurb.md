Sunday, February 18th, 1691
>I had an unsettling vision while I slept last night, and in my efforts to keep the last vestiges of the memory available to me, I have written below the events I remember.
>I am in a place of sharp angles, unnaturally twisted. The winding of the angles is impossible to follow with my eyes. Even trying to remember them makes me dizzy. Odd beings surround me, some shadowy and ethereal, some solid white and black, swirling as ink dropped into water. I smell burning, cloth, and flesh alike. The pungent scent fills my nose and makes it hard to breathe. The figures swarm me, chanting in a language I can't understand. They suddenly vanish in puffs of smoke. A stillness envelops the space, those unnatural corners seeming to dampen all sound.
>The terrible delusion cuts to towering waves, a deep red, violently tossing me about. Each time I open my mouth, a metallic, viscous liquid replaces the air that should be there. Sheer will keeps me afloat, the waves crashing into me, pulling me into the depths. Light fades, and blood fills my lungs, reaching my hand to the surface. The warmth of the blood gets colder as I go deeper. The depths consume me, cold as ice, and I start awake. Sweat and tears have dampened the bed.
>I write this some time later, when Thomas is out again. Night visions fade from the mind, as they are oft to do. I am missing an integral piece, and I do not know what.
>Thomas is gone, a light flickering through the low door frame into the room. Shadows jump around, their tendril assaulting me like the ones from my dream. I'm hot, so hot. I need to escape, go somewhere cool. I stand, legs trembling, and quietly walk outside into the cool, crisp night. I sit for a while. Eventually, gray streaks of light appear, birds singing their songs that herald the dawn and day to come.
>The days come and pass, and I seem to watch myself from outside my body. Thomas offers no comfort, even though I know he is in as much pain as I am. He wears a mask of stoicism for others. I believe he resents me for how the village sees us. Well, how the village sees me, he doesn't take any scrutiny from the other Builders. I see darkness and accusation in his eyes. I pray for the day we heal, but it doesn't seem possible.
>-Sylvia

Tuesday, May 1st, 1691
>The dream continues to plague me. I have been able to fill in some missing parts, though some still escape me. I believe the source of the dream to be my shame and guilt for what has happened. Sweet Charlie deserved a chance, and he got none. And Abigail from the year before. The silence is deafening where I float.  The corners that fold in upon themselves invite my eyes to trace them. The serpentine lines wriggle, playing a game I can never win. I can't tell why the silence is so pervasive. I am being torn apart by the creatures of ink, their claws digging into my skin. My limbs are ripped in opposite directions, my joints protesting in pain. A gale of wind buffets me this way and that, and the thought bubbles to my mind that I have been dropped. The monsters have finally left me alone

I am deafened by the roar of the air, my body flailing wildly, flying like a ragdoll through the wind. I slam into a liquid. When my vision clears, there is an iron tang on filling my mouth, the towering waves forcing me under the surface.
>Each time I awake from that dream, I am distraught. The dream always ends shortly before the first signs of dawn.
>Theresa, Ann, and the others have helped me through my grief. I do think it is they, however, that have started claims about the unnaturalness of the stillbirths. I suppose God works in mysterious ways. The Szent contains a passage in the Book of Hope, Chapter X, verses 10 through 15:
>The Almighty hath created two absolutes for mankind, one each in the image of Him. One, for raising the walls that protect us, the other for tilling the fertile dirt to feed. Henceforth, these will be the Builders and Growers.

>Vezetuu Martin, may he rest in Her embrace, wrote the following after a divine meeting with Her:
>	... and she spoke unto me, filling my eyes and mind with her brightness, "The Grower has but a few functions of society: using their fertile valleys to produce the godly fruit of the future, the children... and grow the crops to nourish us."
> 	"...the green and fertile crop of a valley can fail for a multitude of reasons, and for the dear Growers, it can and will happen. I hath made this a fact of your lives. Have Hope and see the light through the dark when such a crop fails."

>It must be my imagination, but the others seem to blame me for "the darkness." The heartbreak of Charlie brought back the grief I thought I had buried for Abigail as well, tenfold. I am being crushed, buried by the melancholy that encases my heart. My dear husband must know what is eating away at my insides. I see the way he scrutinizes me when he thinks I am not aware. The depths of his brown eyes decry the failed crop I am.
> -Sylvia

Saturday, August 4th, 1691
>Once again, I have heard the whispers and shadows of rumors floating around the village. Growers these days are mostly women. Verbal tales of days long past tell of a time when anyone who wanted to be a Grower could be. Builders and Growers weren't defined by sex. Over time, the men started associating Building with being masculine. The men seem to play a game of pride. I can't remember the last time I saw a Grower who wasn't a woman.

Thursday, November 8th, 1691
>The deafening silence from my night terrors continues to creep up on me throughout the day. What should've been a home and family brimming with warmth, love, and joy is an empty shell. Leagues separate Thomas and me in this small cabin, the frost from without inching its icy tendrils through the gaps in the walls. He seems to be on the town watch more often, even though he detested the duty earlier in the year.

Friday, February 29th, 1692
>The same dream visits me every night. Every night, like clockwork, it visits. Each time is a brand new experience. I can't tell anyone, that will throw fuel on the fire. I can't wait to get away from this place and those accusing eyes.

Saturday, April 5th, 1692

Wednesday, June 4th, 1692
>I've taken to walking in the woods during the day, often staying out after dusk, past boundaries set by the colony. It's peaceful, and the sounds of the forest replace the silence in the cabin and in my heart.

Animals have a simple existence free from the petty politics of the town. He gives me a respite from the predatory gaze of the townsfolk. He's a great listener too, often sitting on my lap and kneading my skin like dough. His only response is a squeak of a meow, or a deep rumble from within his very core. I was lucky enough that my grandmother taught me to write and do math myself. Maybe I can teach Soot to write. The lack of thumbs may present a problem, but I believe in his adroitness.
-Sylvia

Friday, June 13th, 1692
>I write this from the woods well before dawn. Thomas was back from the bar late. Loud as he usually is, it awoke me from the dream. I was grateful, at first, until I snapped out of the grogginess of deep sleep. In his drunken fit, he sat on and broke a chair. I seem unable to complete basic tasks, especially the one set out for me by God. I certainly don't feel like I'm enough.  I occupy more space I am worth, and that is wrong. His eyes are the only eyes to carry empathy. He acknowledges who I am. He sits on my lap, a deep, calm vibration emanating from his core. His little paw is over his nose.
>I notice redcap mushrooms with white dots form a circle around me. The ocean of blood has withdrawn for the night, and the gray streaks of dawn grace the sky. The heavens give their last twinkle before they, too, are drowned by a superior force. Soot awakes and, after looking at me, wanders off to do activities only Soot knows about.
>I must return and see if old Elizabeth, the herbalist, has anything for me. My face is puffy.
>-Sylvia

Tuesday, June 17th, 1692

My feet will dangle above those blocks by the end of the day. I don't care. This will be the sweet release I have been searching for. My only sorrow is that the Lady Moon and Soot might miss my presence.

The Moon accepts me for me. She knows my sorrows as she knows her own. The Church could never accept me as I am now. I broke the mold and was punished for it. My inability to birth children is not who I am.

>Farewell, cruel world, I won't miss you.
